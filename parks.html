Bennett Park/Navin Field (1896-11)/(1912-37)

Named for Charlie Bennett, Detroit catcher who lost his legs in a train accident. Opened with 5,000 seats; home field of the Detroit Tigers of the Western League. Clubhouse in centerfield in play. Demolished in 1911 and replaced by Navin Field. Briggs Stadium replaced Navin in 1938 until it was renamed Tiger Stadium.

Location:
Michigan Ave., Trumbull and Cochrane streets and Fisher.
Current site:
Demolition completed in 2009. Playing field remains.
More:
www.baseball-reference.com, www.ballparktour.com
Columbia Park (1901-08)

Built in 1901 for the Philadelphia Athletics by manager Connie Mack and team President Benjamin Shibe. No dugouts, just wooden benches and only one clubhouse. Opened with a capacity of 9,500, expanded by 4,100 four years later. Replaced by Shibe Park after the 1908 season.

Location:
Cecil B. Moore Ave., 29th, 30th and Oxford streets.
Current site:
Replaced by houses.
More:
www.philadelphiaathletics.org, www.ballparks.com
Shibe Park (renamed Connie Mack Stadium) (1909-54)

Built for Philadelphia Athletics. With 23,000 seats and standing room for 10,000, more than twice the capacity of Columbia Park. Dead center was 515 feet. Phillies shared park starting in 1938.

Location:
Lehigh Ave., 21st, 20th and Somerset streets.
Current site:
A's moved to Kansas City in 1954. Fire destroyed stadium in 1971. Now site of church.
More:
www.ballparksofbaseball.com
Ebbets Field (1913-57)

Built for NL Brooklyn Dodgers by Charlie Ebbets. Site was dump where pigs feasted on garbage, thus the name "Pigstown". Seating for 25,000 (increased to 32,000 in 1932) and a 38-foot-high right field wall 297 feet from home.

Location:
Montgomery St., McKeever Place, Sullivan Place and Bedford Ave. in Brooklyn.
Current site:
Demolished in 1960, three years after Dodgers left for L.A.. Replaced by Ebbets Field Apartments.
More:
www.ebbets-field.com, www.ballparks.com, www.ballparksofbaseball.com
Huntington Avenue Grounds (1901-11)

Before Fenway Park, the Boston Red Sox played at a tiny 11,500-seat park. 635 feet to dead center. A shed in centerfield was in play. Site of first AL-NL World Series in 1903, and first modern-era perfect game, tossed by Cy Young in 1904.

Location:
Huntington Ave., Forsyth Street, New Gravelly Point and railroad tracks.
Current site:
Northeastern University.
More:
www.baseball-alamanc.com
Hilltop Park (1903-12)

New York's first AL club was the transplanted Baltimore Orioles. Called the New York Highlanders, they played at Hilltop Park. Seated 16,000 with standing room for 15,000.

Location:
Fort Washington Ave., 165th and 168th streets and Broadway in Manhattan.
Current site:
Columbia-Presbyterian Medical Center.
More:
www.ballparksofbaseball.com, www.baseball-almanac.com
West Side Grounds (1893-1915)

Where Chicago Cubs legendary double-play combo Tinker-to-Evers-to-Chance played. Cubs appeared in four World Series in five seasons, winning in 1907 and '08. Site of first NL game.

Location:
S. Lincoln Ave. and W. Taylor, S. Wood and W. Polk streets.
Current site:
University of Illinois at Chicago Medical Center.
More:
www.ballparks.com, www.projectballpark.org
South Side Park (1901-10)

Home of Chicago White Sox until Comiskey Park built. Rebuilt after a fire in 1909; Sox moved after 1910 because the park was too small. Nicknamed "the Hitless Wonder" for its expanse.

Location:
W. 37th Street, S. Princeton Ave., W. Pershing Rd. and S. Wentworth Ave.
Current site:
Demolished in late 1940s for public housing.
More:
www.ballparks.com, www.projectballpark.org
League Park (1891-1951)

Home to NL Cleveland Spiders (1891-99) and AL Cleveland Indians (1901-46). Built with 9,000 seats; expanded to 21,414. Site of 3,000th hit for Nap Lajoie and Tris Speaker; Walter Johnson's 3,000th strikeout; Babe Ruth's 500th homer.

Location:
Northeast corner of East 66th St. and Lexington Ave.
Current site:
Park. Part of brick facade and ticket office remain.
More:
www.ballparks.com, www.leaguepark.org
National Park (1892-1911)

Home to Washington Senators of the NL, but when the club went bankrupt, it was replaced with another team of the same name from the upstart AL in 1901. Destroyed by fire in 1911, replaced on site by Griffith Stadium. Location: Georgia Avenue (extension of 7th Ave.), Florida Ave., Bohrer and 5th streets.

Location:
Georgia Avenue (extension of 7th Ave.), Florida Ave., Bohrer and 5th streets.
Current site:
Howard University Hospital.
